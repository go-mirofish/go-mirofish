On October 18, 2026, the Harbor Renewal Office announced that the old ferry terminal in Southpoint would be converted into a temporary floating market and emergency shelter hub after a storm damaged two nearby logistics piers. The announcement sounded routine at first, but the details quickly drew public attention because the site sits between the Riverside neighborhood in Southpoint and the commuter district in North Quay. The city said the pilot would run for ninety days while repairs continued, with a decision due before Lunar New Year.

Mayor Elena Park framed the project as a practical resilience test. Deputy mayor Marcus Vale called it the fastest way to preserve jobs for dock workers, street vendors, and delivery drivers. The Harbor Renewal Office said the design team from Meridian Civic Lab would install modular stalls, battery storage, and medical tents within ten days. Portwatch Union leader Ravi Menon welcomed the emergency funding but warned that docking access for repair barges must not be blocked. Blue Current Logistics, which leases a warehouse near the terminal, asked for guaranteed truck windows so freight vehicles could still reach North Quay before sunrise.

The first criticism came from the Riverside Families Association, led by Tessa Huang, which argued that the city had skipped a public hearing and underestimated noise after midnight. School principal Jonah Ellis at Riverside Secondary said morning congestion already backed up onto Shoreline Avenue and feared the market would delay buses. Community organizer Alma Rios from the Southpoint Mutual Aid Network supported the shelter component but said the city was blending disaster response with commercial activity in a way that could displace low-income residents. She insisted that any vendor permits prioritize local cooperatives and not outside chains.

Three private groups then escalated the debate. Lantern Foods proposed a branded night market zone with celebrity chefs and livestream programming. North Quay Chamber of Commerce chair Priya Desai supported the idea, claiming it could revive tourism after a slow quarter. Meanwhile, civic watchdog group Open Ledger Coalition, represented by journalist Ben Carter, questioned why two subcontract awards went to firms with prior consulting ties to Meridian Civic Lab. Their request for procurement records was amplified by local station Harbor Radio and by the student newsroom at Riverside Secondary.

On October 22, a leaked slide deck circulated online. It suggested the Harbor Renewal Office had modeled a larger expansion footprint reaching two side streets and planned to collect foot traffic data through Wi-Fi sensors. Council member Sofia Bennett, whose district includes North Quay, called for an emergency hearing. Council member Adrian Cole said the pilot should continue but only if the city publishes safety, privacy, and revenue-sharing rules. Chief resilience officer Naomi Ibarra responded that the slide deck was an early draft and not an approved operational plan.

By the weekend, public sentiment had split into clear camps. Portwatch Union, Southpoint Mutual Aid Network, Blue Current Logistics, Riverside Families Association, Meridian Civic Lab, Lantern Foods, Open Ledger Coalition, the Harbor Renewal Office, and the North Quay Chamber of Commerce were all issuing statements. Crowds gathered outside city hall in Southpoint, while business owners in North Quay posted competing petitions. The city now faces a chain reaction of concerns about transparency, shelter access, transport disruption, labor guarantees, privacy, procurement fairness, and whether a short-term emergency pilot is quietly becoming a permanent redevelopment strategy.
